The thesis

POP may not have a rewards problem.
It may have a reward legibility problem —

the value of POPcoins may not become visible to a new user fast enough to convert UPI frequency into a shopping habit.

Framing

My outside-in assumptions, stated plainly.

Four things I'm taking as given before the rest of the note runs. If any of these are wrong, the recommendations downstream change shape.

01

My standing

I am an active POP user. This note is an outside-in hypothesis — not an internal audit, and I have no access to metrics or roadmap.

02

The loop is sound

POP's loop — pay, earn, understand, shop, return — is structurally sound. The question is whether each handoff in the loop is visible at the right moment.

03

The real competitor

POP is not only competing with UPI apps. It is competing with the user's mental model of what a reward is worth, right now, before they have experienced the ecosystem.

04

The constraint I'm holding

None of the recommendations require increasing reward spend. The goal is to make existing value more legible — not to create new value from scratch.

Gap 01 · Value abstraction
"20 POPcoins" lands as a token, not a price.

The user may not immediately feel what 20 coins means in shopping terms. The rupee equivalence exists in the product but isn't always felt in the moment of earning.

Gap 02 · Discovery friction
Peak attention, but no on-ramp.

After payment the user is at peak attention. Getting to POPshop still requires a deliberate navigation step — the connection between earning and spending is not automatic.

Gap 03 · No repeat trigger
Visit two starts at zero.

After a first purchase there is no visible reason the shop gets easier or more relevant on the second visit. The loop restarts from scratch rather than from a remembered state.

Framework

Three principles before the recommendations.

Each principle answers a separate "when" or "where" question. The recommendations on the next three slides are direct expressions of these.

I.

Show value at the moment of earning — not on a separate screen.

Coins should feel spendable the second they appear, not after the user navigates to a help section or opens the shop independently. The earn moment and the value explanation should be one event.

II.

Make the post-payment screen do double duty.

It currently closes the transaction. It could also open the shopping loop. The user's attention is already there — this is the lowest-cost entry point into commerce POP has.

III.

Make POPshop answer "what do I do with my coins right now."

Not "what does POP sell." Intent-led surfacing does not require a new catalogue. It requires a different entry point to the same one.

POPshop intent shelves.

Same catalogue. Five intent-led shelves on entry.

Each shelf is one question the user is silently asking when they tap into POPshop.

  1. 01
    "Best use of your coins today."

    Products where coin coverage is highest relative to the user's current balance.

  2. 02
    "Under ₹X after your coins."

    An effective-price filter using the actual balance, not the listed price.

  3. 03
    "Deals added since your last visit."

    Novelty — makes return visits feel worthwhile, no recommendation engine needed.

  4. 04
    "Trending with POP users this week."

    Social proof. No social graph required.

  5. 05
    "Deals expiring soon."

    Urgency that's factual, not manufactured.

Hypothesis check

Is the wide catalogue the problem? My hypothesis: no. The issue is that a broad catalogue entered without context feels random. Intent shelves give the same products a reason to exist for the user in this session.

Recommendation 03 of 03

A lightweight repeat-visit layer.

A shop that remembers you is harder to leave than one that doesn't. This does not require a recommendation engine — it requires saving a small amount of state between sessions.

Three things to surface on the second visit.

Resume
"Continue where you left off."

Last browsed category or product. One tile. No personalisation engine.

Balance-aware
"Your coin balance improved — these items are cheaper now."

Dynamic. Re-computed against the wishlist or recently-browsed set.

Cross-signal
"Deals in categories you've used recently."

Derived from payment history, not shopping history.

Switching cost, accrued

Each visit deposits into a familiarity layer. Visit two is easier than one. Visit three easier than two. By visit four, a competitor needs equivalent history to feel as good.

Bonus framing

Consider surfacing a single "Your POP Price" — the effective price after maximum coins. Keep the coin-plus-cash breakdown one tap away, not the headline.

Open questions

Five things I'd like to understand from the inside.

These are genuine questions, not critiques. My outside-in view has limits — and I'd learn more from the internal perspective on each of these.

Q1
On coin applicability

What determines which products allow higher coin coverage vs. lower? My guess: merchant-funded caps, category margins, and campaign budgets — but what other variables are in play, and how visible is that logic to the user today?

Q2
On UPI → shopping conversion

At what point in the journey does the shift from UPI user to POPshop user typically happen? Does it correlate with payment frequency, coin-balance thresholds, or a specific merchant category? This would help prioritise where to intervene first.

Q3
On payment-to-shop signals

Is there a relationship between what a user pays for via UPI and what they browse in POPshop? If that signal exists, is it currently being used to surface deals — and if not, is the constraint data or product?

Q4
On card conversion

Does POPshop engagement predict credit-card conversion, or is the card path largely independent of shopping behaviour? Understanding this clarifies whether improving the shopping loop is also improving the LTV path.

Q5
On merchant deal consistency

How does deal depth and duration vary across merchant partners, and does that variation affect how users perceive the shop over time? Inconsistency in available deals could be a hidden retention factor worth tracking.

Final takeaway

POP's model has a structural advantage that most cashback products do not — coins have a destination, a merchant network, and a card attached to them.

That's harder to replicate than a 2% cashback rate — and it compounds with time.

The hypothesis, in one paragraph

The loop may benefit from being made slightly more visible at each handoff — not through a product rebuild, but through a set of framing and connection changes that can be tested quickly and cheaply. If the hypothesis holds, the improvement should appear in retention metrics within 30 days, and in card conversion over the following quarter.

Closing

I'm genuinely interested in understanding how these questions look from the inside, and in contributing to the thinking around them. The note is short on purpose — happy to go deeper on any single slide.
